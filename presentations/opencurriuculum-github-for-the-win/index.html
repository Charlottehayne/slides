Two things about the OpenTechSchool

The OpenTechSchool is an open movement started in April 2012 in Berlin trying to educate people on technology by organising hands-on coding- and tech-related workshops and learners events.

Our focus is on empowering people by providing hands-on learning experiences in a open and safe environment and enabling others to do the same by providing an open documentation of our doing.

We are just the coach

A short introduction to our most used learning model

Introduction to Coaching

In OTS we do not teach, we coach. Meaning there is no one in front at our workshops but you work through the curriculum yourself at your own pace or in small groups of peers while there are experts around to help you out whenever needed - we call them "the coaches on the side lines".

New learning material needed

This learning style requires other material than most of what is out there. And we - as the OpenTechSchool - had some more requirements to them:

  • Easily writable
  • Public and open accessible
  • Easy to contribute to
  • Easy and cheap to host and to maintain

GitHub-Pages

Static HTML-Pages with Git at their heart

Opening a bunch of possbilities

  • Comfortable and easy collaborative writing
  • Local but published and accesssible changes via forks
  • Contributing via Pull-Requests (German Version)
  • Published online quickly and forever

Easy writing

  • Writing simple markdown or rest Text files
  • Using well established tools like Git and Github for collaboration
  • everyone can use their most favourite editor

Forking

  • make your own copy to work on
  • directly share it online with others
  • work with others on material not "yet official"

Pull Requests

And once your part is done, you make a "pull request" to the main repository and it gets merged into it with just a click.

Hosted online for free and forever

And just seconds after you've pushed your content, it is available online. Hosted for free and forever online.

But there is more

Fixes on Premise

Quickly fixing the material during the workshop via the github online editor and ask the participatants to hit refresh to distribute it way before most of them noticed the mistake. Priceless!

Tracking Issues on Premise

Tracking your bugs directly while working with the learners online so that you don't forget to fix them later. Again: open and public.

So, everyone is free to fix them. Make a pull request and get stuff fixed.

Conclusion

Only Plaintext is Open

  • Content is key
  • Rendering is secondary
  • WYSIWIGs suck
  • and are unportable
  • even Wordpress
  • and GoogleDocs

But what about wikiX?

Wikis often suffer a bad design in both visual and structural ways. Just think about how to organise learners material in "categories".

Wikis focus on speed. Quality comes second: first you publish than someone reviews (or often doesn't).

On learners material quality is more important than speed: only after reviewed by an equal peer a pull-request gets merged and the content is published.
